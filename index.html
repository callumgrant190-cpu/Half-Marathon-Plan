What each literature says

Gaelic football

As a full-back you sit at the lower end of match running volume (full-back/full-forward lines run least; midfielders most), but you're still exposed to repeated sprints — and sprint distance drops ~33% from first to second half, more than any other metric, meaning repeat-sprint durability matters. The takeaway: your sport gives you sprint exposure for free, but under-trains pure acceleration force for a defender. That's the gap the summer block fills.

Half marathon

Cohort data on 556 half-marathoners (van Poppel et al.) found weekly volume >32km and long runs >21km both linked to faster times with no rise in injury risk — so the October block doesn't artificially cap mileage. Threshold is the single biggest determinant, and ~80% of volume stays easy (Seiler). None of this overlaps with GAA's demands, which is exactly why you periodise them in sequence, not together.

Australian Rules — the bridge between the two

AFL players carry a big aerobic engine and repeat-sprint ability at once — the exact combination you're chasing. Two findings drive this plan: (1) higher aerobic qualities are matched with greater match distance and ball involvement, so building your base should help your GAA; and (2) high-speed-running volume varies enormously between individuals, so sprint load must be individually monitored — which is the evidence base for connecting your Whoop/Garmin/Strava rather than following fixed numbers blindly.

Your profile shapes this

21, 180cm, 84kg. Full-back / occasional half-back. Four hamstring injuries including a grade-3 tear and a tendinopathy. That history drives most of the specific choices.

Sprinting is protective, not just risky

A U-shaped relationship — players who never reach ≥95% of max speed are at higher hamstring-injury risk than those exposed regularly (a "vaccine effect"). The danger is months of avoidance then a sudden flat-out return. Your GAA matches keep that exposure alive in-season; the Oct block keeps it via weekly sprint maintenance.

Acceleration is the safer speed quality

Max-velocity sprinting loads the hamstring hardest in lengthened late-swing — where your injuries happen. Falling starts and short accelerations keep you forward-leaning and glute/quad-dominant, a more protected position. That's a key reason the summer focus is acceleration, not top-end speed.

Nordics are non-negotiable

Eccentric strength protects you precisely because it's maintained consistently. In a heavy week, halve the dose (2 sets not 4) rather than skip. The sprint exposure + eccentric strength + technique combination cuts risk far more than any one alone.

At 21 you're in the ideal training-age bracket — fast CNS recovery, can handle faster progression than older athletes, so the conservative ramp has headroom if you're adapting well. Given the grade-3 history, get a baseline eccentric-strength check and a word with your physio/S&C before pushing into the heavy acceleration work and (later) max-velocity phases.

CNS load & the high-low principle

Borrowed from Charlie Francis — with one honest caveat.

Francis's signature rule is avoiding the "dead zone" (~75–95% of max speed): too fast for recovery, too slow for max-speed adaptation.

The tension

A strict reading would flag your threshold/tempo work as exactly that dead zone — and on his terms he's right. But for a half marathon, that zone is the target: lactate threshold is the biggest determinant of your race. So the Oct block keeps the work Francis would cut. What carries over from him: treat threshold as a real CNS stressor (not stackable), respect the 48–72hr hamstring/CNS window for true speed, and keep heavy lifting simple and low-volume.

The summer acceleration block actually sits closer to pure Francis logic — short, maximal, fully-recovered, no dead-zone tempo. It's the cleaner speed-development window, which is another reason to do it then rather than during the endurance block.

Why this sequencing

  • Speed before endurance. Build acceleration in summer while fresh and sprint-primed; protect it through the endurance block rather than trying to build it under high mileage.
  • Let the sport do the work. In-season, matches supply sprint exposure — so added training is acceleration quality + easy aerobic, never extra max-velocity on a match week.
  • Monitor, don't guess. The AFL literature shows sprint load varies hugely between individuals — hence device-driven session adjustment.
  • Volume isn't the enemy. >32km/week and >21km long runs link to faster half times with no injury-risk rise; the real ceiling is your week-to-week hamstring response.

Practical notes

  • If a week feels off, repeat it rather than push forward.
  • Fuel and hydrate around long runs and quality/acceleration sessions.
  • The Oct-block time trials (wk 7 & 14) make race pace real, not guessed.
  • Any hamstring tightness, twinge or asymmetry — during a run, an acceleration effort, Nordics or plyo — is a stop-and-reassess signal, not push-through.

With your commitment, the limiting factor is patience with the buildup — not effort.
